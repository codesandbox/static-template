About Gouda

Named after a Dutch town, gouda has a mild, slightly nutty flavor; an elegant substitute for cheddar.

Gouda


Gouda, named after the town in the Netherlands where it was traditionally traded, is one of the oldest recorded cheeses still made today, with its first mention in the late 12th Century. It's made from cow's milk in large rounds with a thin rind. Gouda has a light, mild flavor that varies slightly depending on how long it's aged--generally from four weeks to over a year. It can also be smoked during ageing. It's extremely versatile and one of the most popular cheeses world-wide.

About Manchego

This hard cheese from Spain has a buttery texture and a tangy, earthy, sweet flavor; perfect in dishes with ham, honey, or eggs.

Manchego


This Spanish cheese is made from milk of the Manchega breed of sheep and aged from 60 days to two years. It ranges in color from white to pale ivory and has a delightfully earthy, fruity flavor that gets stronger with age. Its thin, inedible rind is characterized by a distinctive zig-zag pattern. Eat it on its own or on tapas as part of a delectible meal!

About Havarti

This semi-hard cheese hailing from Denmark has a sumptous, buttery texture and flavor that goes well with crackers or in a sandwich.

Havarti


Originating in Denmark, this semi-hard cheese is generally made in blocks or rectangular loaves that are aged for around three months. It has no rind and is usually a soft white color. Its deliciously creamy, buttery texture and flavor make it a favorite for snacking, sandwiches, or melting on just about anything. Try the variety with dill for an exta special sensory delight!

About Honey Goat

As the name implies, this cheese blends tangy chevre goat cheese and sweet honey for a delightful soft cheese to spread on toast or dollop on a salad.

Honey Goat


Honey goat cheese, as the name implies, is the combination of goat cheese, or chevre (from the French word for goat) and honey. Many grocery stores sell this as a single offering (generally in the form of small logs or medallians) or you can make it yourself at home by softening the chevre and blending in honey to taste. The combination of tangy,salty chevre and sweet honey will have your tastebuds asking for more. Goat cheese itself has been made for thousands of years and comes in many forms. Honey goat is ideal for spreading on crostini topped with berries or on a tortilla to elevate a veggie wraps.

About Burrata

This soft Italian cheese is a ball of mozzerella filled with a melty, creamy center. The combination is pure bliss!

Burrata


This devinely creamy cheese from Italy is made with cow's milk or buffalo mozzerella that is formed into a small ball while warm and then stuffed with cream and stringy mozzarella or stracciatella scraps. It's quickly wrapped to preserve its freshness. When cut open, the creamy center oozes out. Burrata is delicious on salads, pasta, or in a caprese salad (with tomato, fresh basil leaves, and olive oil). Combined with a ilttle pesto and some crusty bread, it also makes a particularly decadent grilled cheese sandwich.

About Dubliner

This hard cheese hailing from Ireland has a sharp flavor reminiscent of cheddar. Pair it with apple slices for a sweet and savory snack.

Dubliner


It's no surprise that this hard cheese made from cow's milk hails from Ireland, although it's actually made in Cork, not Dublin. Invented in the 1990s, Dubliner is aged for 12 - 24 months. It has a sharp but sweet taste and a slightly crumbly texture. Use it anywhere you'd use cheddar but want more of a hit to the tastebuds.

How to Make the Perfect Cheeseboard

The best cheeseboards feature more than just cheese and crakers. A good cheeseboard teases your palette, combining complementary flavors and textures to create a party in your mouth with every bite.

The ideal cheese board has six elements:

  • 3-4 distinct cheeses (e.g., hard/soft, sweet/sharp, cow/goat)
  • Crackers or crostini (avoid crackers like Ritz or Wheat Thins, which have a strong flavor profile)
  • Fruit, such as grapes, dates, or apricots
  • Something crunchy, like pistachios or cornichons
  • Something salty, like meat (salami slices and proscuito work particularly well) or green olives
  • A sweet spread, such as quince paste or blackberry jam

Experiment with various flavor profiles and texture combinations in each board, making cheese the star of the show. No matter what you choose, you and your guests are sure to come back for seconds and thirds, because who can resist cheese?

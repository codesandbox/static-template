From rose petals to origami flowers

Preface

By its very definition, origami is the folding of paper into a shape or form, and paper is of course made from plant materials. This can also include various types of flowers and its petals, which already possess natural colours and patterns that are more beautiful than what we are able to reproduce on paper. So while it might not seem like an obvious starting material, for a discerning origamist it may very well be the most logical choice.

The idea of making origami flowers from flower petals may sound like an exercise in futility and frustration, since turning a flower from its natural form to an origami model is a demanding task that doesn’t necessarily increase its aesthetic appeal. Yet like many artisans who choose to hone their skills on a seemingly impossible task, the purpose for undertaking the task isn’t simply about being able to complete it.

Previous enquiries made to verify if others had attempted or achieved this feat failed to turn up any substantial information or leads. However, one cannot dismiss the notion that there are others who have also successfully tackled this exercise, even if there are no florists or origamists that have come forward to verify their attempts. The information documented below is a brief description of how to use flower petals for origami, which acknowledges the fact that not every flower is going to be suitable for a particular type of origami model, and that success is never a guarantee when working with such delicate and organic raw materials. However, what is certain is that you will gain a much greater appreciation for both flora and origami in the process.

Summary

Preparing a flower petal into a material suitable for origami essentially involves the removal of existing moisture so that it can hold its shape when creased. This also requires you to press the flower petal so it becomes more pliable but retains enough moisture so that it is not too brittle when folded. Therefore the origami model you decide to make determines the type of flower used, and the preparation required (and vice versa). Due to the various factors that impact the drying process of flower petals (e.g. temperature, humidity, size of petals, etc.) the exact timing is difficult to estimate precisely without a controlled environment. In addition, this is a summary of the general process for a rose petal but different types of flower will require adjustments to the factors and variables that will be described in more detail.

Selecting the flower

The primary considerations in selecting the types of flowers to use for origami are based on the physical characteristics of the flowers, as determined by the size, shape and thickness of the flowers. Since larger flowers tend to also have a thicker and a more fibrous composition (as well as more prominent veins running through the petal), they become more brittle and hence more difficult to fold when pressed and dried. Interestingly enough, rose petals seem to have an optimal size and thickness when it comes to making miniature origami cranes, as do certain types of orchids. Pick petals that are larger and have less curvature as it will reduce the available surface for folding and minimize the distortion of its shape when pressed into a flat sheet.

Drying the petals

Make sure that all moisture from the surface of the petals is removed by air drying them first. You can use a tissue or a cloth to absorb excessive dew or moisture from the petals before air drying. It is advisable to press dry the flowers using paper that is thick and absorbent (such as blotting paper) and to place some weight on top to help the petals retain a flat and smooth surface. You may also need to change the paper because the moisture retained in the paper used to press the petals can cause discolouration of the petals as part of its natural decomposition process.

Even if the flower petals are already quite flat you should still press them, because in the process of drying they can sometimes lose their original shape/form. A slow and gradual dehydration process, which can be obtained using devices such as dehumidifiers or materials like desiccants is also possible but may require a more specialized set up.

Examining/testing the petals

Due to the different size of flower petals and the temperature or humidity of the surrounding, you may need to regularly check the progress of drying over a number of weeks. The most reliable way for determining when the petals are dry enough is to take a small sample from the same batch of flower petals you are drying and try to make a crease. If the crease does not hold its shape or it feels very soft in the fingertips then it is probably still too moist. On the other hand, if folding the petal causes it to tear apart then it is too dry. Another way to tell if the flower petal is suitable for folding is to see if you can leave fingerprints on the petals when you press your finger onto the surface. When there is a small trace of moisture and pliability, It should leave a very faint impression of your fingerprint, but if it is very smooth and glossy it indicates that the petal is likely to be too fragile and brittle.

Cutting the petals

Use a very sharp knife or scalpel to cut the flower petals into the shape and size of the paper you need for the origami model. Try and pick areas that don’t have a lot of, or have very thick veins because it can be more difficult to cut or fold.

Making the origami model

This is where your skill and patience will be tested, as the paper will be delicate and not easy to work with even when it is optimally dried. However, if you are careful with the pre-creases you will find that there is less stress and pressure put on the model and you are less likely to cause tears at the creases.

If you suspect that the flower petal is going to be too dry or brittle to work with, it is possible to try and moisten the material with a very tiny bit of water and let it soak into the petal. You can then test the consistency of the material by making a pre-crease to see if it is still too dry to fold, or pressing your finger onto the surface to see if the petal is too wet to retain the crease when you fold it.
